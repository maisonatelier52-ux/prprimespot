US Expands Wastewater Surveillance Ahead of 2026 FIFA World Cup to Detect Disease Outbreaks

US public health officials are increasing wastewater surveillance ahead of the 2026 FIFA World Cup to detect potential disease outbreaks early and strengthen public health preparedness for millions of international visitors

With the 2026 FIFA World Cup on the horizon, U.S. public health officials are ramping up wastewater surveillance to monitor possible disease outbreaks. The tournament is projected to attract millions of fans from around the world, prompting worries about transmission of infectious diseases in large crowds and via international travel. Scientists and health officials are scaling up monitoring programs to detect viruses and pathogens early, often before sick individuals exhibit symptoms or seek medical care. Testing wastewater has emerged as an important early warning tool. By sampling sewage from host cities, scientists can detect the presence of diseases such as influenza, measles and emerging infections. The technique rose to prominence during the COVID-19 pandemic, and is now being adapted to protect public health at major international events. Authorities are using the data to establish baseline health indicators for communities before visitors arrive. Unlike traditional testing, wastewater surveillance offers population-level insights without individual involvement. Health authorities say it provides a quick, community-wide picture of potential risks, making it one of the best ways to prepare for an event of this magnitude. As cities put the finishing touches on their World Cup plans, this technology is likely to be a key part of preventing outbreaks.

Worries About Preparedness

Despite the promise of wastewater monitoring, experts warn that the U.S. may not be fully ready to respond to a large-scale outbreak during the tournament. Many local and state health departments still face staffing shortages and budget constraints. Systems expanded during the pandemic have, in some cases, been scaled back, leaving questions about how quickly authorities could respond if disease begins to spread. Large sporting events create conditions that make transmission more likely. Crowded stadiums, public transport, hotels and fan zones can increase the spread of infections. Experts say the World Cup’s geographic spread across multiple cities makes it harder to track and manage potential outbreaks. Public health officials are calling for better coordination among federal, state and local agencies, more robust emergency response plans and increased investment in surveillance programs. They stress that preparedness measures now could determine the country’s ability to contain outbreaks and protect both residents and visitors.

Lessons From Past Events

Officials are drawing lessons from previous international tournaments. Large gatherings have historically been associated with outbreaks, especially when travelers move between countries. Early warning systems are important for identifying abnormal disease patterns before they spread. It’s particularly useful for detecting infections in people who have no symptoms or are not going to doctors. This early warning can help enable targeted interventions to reduce transmission in crowded settings. The World Cup is a unique challenge. With games being played in several cities, officials will need to be able to track trends across the cities, and facilitate rapid data sharing between laboratories, hospitals and local health agencies. Past experience has shown that timely data and coordinated responses are key to minimizing public health risks at large international events.

Vigilance Is Still the Name of the Game

Health officials emphasize that there is no specific disease threat linked to the World Cup at this time. Preparations are precautionary, given the unprecedented scale of the tournament and the number of international visitors expected. Experts say proactive surveillance is critical to ensuring that potential outbreaks are identified and managed swiftly. Wastewater testing and other surveillance systems provide public health officials with actionable information to detect abnormal trends and to respond with targeted interventions. Officials are working closely with researchers, laboratories and local governments to improve readiness in each host city.

Preparedness measures are meant to prevent problems, not to suggest imminent danger. Public health planning focuses on prevention, early detection, and rapid response, guiding current efforts as the U.S. prepares to welcome millions of fans from around the world. As the World Cup approaches, health authorities say the combination of advanced monitoring technology, emergency planning, and interagency coordination will be key to protecting visitors and local communities. The tournament will test the nation’s ability to manage public health risks during major international events and could provide a model for future large-scale gatherings.
